The Big Picture

This is not a technology story. It is a business story.

AI adoption among US small and medium businesses grew from 6% in 2019 to 47% in 2024. That is not a slow trend — it is a structural shift that is accelerating.

Revenue grows faster

Businesses that adopted AI reported 18% higher productivity and significantly faster revenue growth compared to non-adopting competitors, consistently across all industries.

The window is closing

Early adopters are building a three-to-five year lead over competitors who wait. By 2028, two in three small businesses will use AI. The question is no longer whether — it is how.

Leadership matters most

A CEO with a technology background makes a company 2.8 times more likely to adopt AI, regardless of revenue or headcount. Mindset — not budget — is the primary driver.

Digital foundations first

Digital maturity is the single strongest predictor of AI success. Businesses comfortable with cloud tools adopt AI faster, spend less on implementation, and see results sooner.

Key Finding

Digital maturity predicts AI adoption more than size or revenue

Our Gradient Boosting model identified that how digitally advanced a business already is — its maturity score — is the single strongest predictor of whether it will adopt AI.

This means the path to AI runs through digital basics: cloud migration, data strategy, and technology investment come first.

  • Cloud adoption precedes AI adoption in 89% of cases
  • A formal data strategy doubles the likelihood of AI adoption
  • Revenue matters less than how it is invested in technology

The AI Revolution in Small Business America

What five years of data across 5,000 companies tell us about how — and why — small businesses in the United States are embracing artificial intelligence, and what it means for owners and investors alike.

Not long ago, artificial intelligence was something you read about in science fiction or heard mentioned at tech conferences. It felt distant — a tool for giant corporations with unlimited budgets, not for the local accounting firm, the family-owned manufacturer, or the neighborhood pharmacy.

That story has changed dramatically.

From 6% to 47% in five years

In 2019, only 6 out of every 100 small businesses were using any form of artificial intelligence. By 2024, that number jumped to 47 — almost half. Two events reshaped the landscape entirely.

The COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 forced millions of businesses to go digital almost overnight. The launch of ChatGPT in late 2022 was the single biggest turning point: suddenly AI was no longer abstract. It was a tool you could open in a browser, type a question to, and get a useful answer from — without hiring a data scientist or spending millions of dollars.

AI Adoption Rate Among US Small Businesses, 2019–2024

Each data point represents the share of surveyed US SMEs using at least one AI tool that year. The inflection after 2022 reflects the ChatGPT effect.

Not all industries are moving at the same pace

Technology companies, financial firms, and healthcare providers are racing ahead. By 2024, nearly all technology businesses had adopted some form of AI. At the other end, agriculture, construction, and hospitality businesses are moving much more slowly. The gap between the fastest and slowest sectors was nearly 90 percentage points in 2024.

AI Adoption by Industry — 2024

Longer bars indicate higher adoption. The gap between Technology (97%) and Agriculture (20%) highlights how unevenly the AI revolution is arriving.

"The businesses that move in the next two years will have a three-to-five year head start on those who wait until it becomes unavoidable. In competitive markets, that gap can be decisive."

The one thing that predicts AI adoption more than anything else

Digital maturity — how advanced a business already is in its use of digital tools — is the single strongest predictor of AI success. Think of it like a staircase: AI is not the first step. It is closer to the top. Before a business can realistically benefit from AI, it generally needs to be comfortable with digital tools in general.

The businesses that adopted cloud computing first were far more likely to adopt AI next. A CEO with a technology background makes a company 2.8 times more likely to adopt, regardless of size or revenue.

What the future looks like

By 2027, our models suggest 67 out of every 100 small businesses will be using AI. By 2028, that climbs to 71%. The businesses that adopt earlier tend to gain advantages that compound over time. The gap between early adopters and those who wait tends to widen, not close.

"AI adoption among US small businesses grew 7x in five years. By 2028, two out of three small businesses will use AI in some form. The window for early-mover advantage is still open — but it is closing."
